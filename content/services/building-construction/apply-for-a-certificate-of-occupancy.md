# Apply for a Certificate of Occupancy — City of Koronadal

A Certificate of Occupancy (Occupancy Permit) is the document the **Office of the Building Official (OBO)** issues once your building is finished and proven fit for use. It confirms the structure complies with the National Building Code of the Philippines (PD 1096) and matches your approved plans, so you can legally occupy and use it.

## How to Apply

### Step 1: Submit your documents at the OBO

- Bring your complete requirements to the Office of the Building Official.
- Staff receive and check your documents for completeness and authenticity (about 10 minutes).

### Step 2: Endorsement to the City Assessor's Office

- The OBO endorses your issued building permit plans to the **City Assessor's Office** to validate the latest tax declaration for the building.

### Step 3: Site inspection and technical validation

- An inspection team conducts a site inspection and validates your submitted documents for Line & Grade, Architectural, Structural, Electrical, Mechanical, Sanitary, Plumbing, and Electronics requirements.

### Step 4: Get your Order of Payment

- If approved, the OBO computes your fees and prepares an Order of Payment.
- If there are deficiencies, you receive a list and your documents back to correct and resubmit. (A returned application cuts off the processing clock; resubmission starts a new period.)

### Step 5: Pay at the City Treasurer's Office

- Pay the assessed fees per the New Schedule of Fees, then keep your Official Receipt.

### Step 6: Claim your Certificate of Occupancy

- Present your Official Receipt at the OBO. After endorsement to the City Assessor for the building tax declaration, the office prepares and issues your Certificate of Occupancy.

## Requirements

### Documents Needed

- [ ] Duly filled-up Unified Application Form (Certificate of Occupancy and Fire Safety Inspection Certificate), signed and sealed by the professional and owner (4 copies)
- [ ] Duly filled-up and notarized Certificate of Completion, signed and sealed (4 copies)
- [ ] Duly filled-up Certificate of Final Electrical Inspection, signed by the involved professional (4 copies)
- [ ] Proof of Ownership over the lot/property; for representatives, a notarized SPA or Authorization Letter with valid IDs
- [ ] If the professional signing the COC/CFEI differs from the one on the building/ancillary permits: notarized Affidavit of Change of Engineer or Waiver of Rights
- [ ] Issued Building Permit plans and forms (2 photocopies)
- [ ] Issued Fire Safety Correction Sheet and its corresponding Fire Safety Evaluation Clearance (1 photocopy)
- [ ] Valid PRC ID and current Professional Tax Receipt (PTR) with specimen signatures and seals of all involved professionals
- [ ] Fire Safety Inspection Certificate (FSIC) from the Bureau of Fire Protection (apply via www.fsis.e-bfp.com)
- [ ] Colored photos of the completed structure showing front, sides, and rear (2 copies)
- [ ] Construction Logbook signed and sealed by the professional and owner (1 original)
- [ ] Expanded envelopes (2 pieces) — green for residential, yellow for commercial, blue for institutional
- [ ] Duly filled-up and notarized Sworn Statement of the True Current and Fair Market Value of Real Property (4 originals)
- [ ] If any recommendations or changes were made during construction: a compliant As-Built Plan reflecting them, signed and sealed (4 sets)

### Eligibility

- Open to all owners of completed buildings and their duly authorized representatives.

## Fees

Certificate of Occupancy fees are computed under the National Building Code's **New Schedule of Fees and Other Charges**. The charter does not state a single flat amount; the OBO will assess and prepare your Order of Payment after evaluation and inspection. Confirm the exact amount with the office before paying.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: When should I apply for a Certificate of Occupancy?**
A: After your building is completed. You will need the notarized Certificate of Completion, Certificate of Final Electrical Inspection, and a Fire Safety Inspection Certificate, among other documents.

**Q: How long does it take?**
A: Like the Building Permit, it depends on classification — 3 working days for simple applications, 7 for complex, and up to 20 for highly technical.

**Q: What if my actual construction changed from the approved plans?**
A: Submit a compliant As-Built Plan reflecting all recommendations and changes, signed and sealed by the involved professional and owner.

**Q: Do I need a Fire Safety Inspection Certificate?**
A: Yes. The FSIC from the Bureau of Fire Protection is part of the checklist; apply through www.fsis.e-bfp.com.
